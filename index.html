What Has a Neural Representation Actually Learned? A Theory of Representation Invariants (TRI) - Release I: Invariant Information Decomposition

Status Foundational release Scope Invariant / variant decomposition Series I of N

Abstract

Modern representation learning has produced highly effective models across vision, language, and multimodal domains. Yet one question remains unresolved:

Given a trained encoder, what invariant information has it actually learned, and what has it irreversibly discarded?

Existing evaluation methods - downstream accuracy, linear probing, representation similarity, and transfer learning, measure the utility of a representation but not its internal invariant structure.

This work introduces the foundational idea behind the Theory of Representation Invariants (TRI): every representation admits a canonical decomposition into an Invariant Core, capturing all transformation-stable information preserved by the encoder, and a complementary Variant Component, containing everything non-invariant. The decomposition is representation-centric, independent of downstream tasks or evaluation protocols. Rather than asking what a representation achieves, TRI asks what it preserves.

§1

Introduction and Motivation

Representation learning underlies modern AI systems in vision, language, speech, and multimodal learning. Despite strong empirical performance, the internal structure of learned representations remains poorly understood.

Current evaluation paradigms focus on external behavior: downstream accuracy, linear probing, transfer learning performance, representation similarity (CKA / CCA), and retrieval metrics. These are useful, but they answer a narrower question -

How useful is a representation for a task?

- rather than the question this work is concerned with:

What invariant information is preserved by the representation itself?

To address this, we introduce a transformation-based view of representations.

Invariant information refers to properties of the input that remain stable under a class of transformations relevant to the data-generating process- e.g. rotation, color shift, viewpoint changes, or noise.

This motivates a structural shift from task-centric evaluation to invariance-centric analysis.

§2

Problem Formulation

2.1 - Setup

  • \(X\) input space
  • \(Z\) representation space
  • \(f\) trained encoder, \(f: X \to Z\)
  • \(\mathcal{T}\) set of transformations acting on \(X\)

Each transformation \(T \in \mathcal{T}\) maps inputs as \(x \mapsto T(x)\).

2.2 - Representation Invariance

A representation is invariant to a transformation \(T\) if

\[ f(x) = f(Tx) \]

or approximately,

\[ d(f(x), f(Tx)) \approx 0 \]

This defines an equivalence structure over the input space induced by the encoder.

2.3 - Key Question

Given a representation \(f\), which aspects of \(X\) remain stable under all transformations in \(\mathcal{T}\), as observed through \(f\)?

§4

Central Idea

We hypothesize that every representation admits a decomposition into two complementary components: invariant information and variant information.

The Invariant Core (\(\phi_f\)) captures all transformation-stable information encoded by \(f\). The Variant Component (\(\nu\)) captures the residual, non-invariant information.

§5

Main Result - Invariant Information Decomposition Theorem

For every measurable representation

\[ f: X \to Z \]

there exists a representation \(\phi_f\), called the Invariant Core, such that:

  1. Factorization. The representation factors through the invariant core.
  2. Maximality. Any invariant function of \(X\) that factors through \(f\) also factors through \(\phi_f\).
  3. Canonical decomposition. \( f \cong (\phi_f, \nu) \), where \(\nu\) contains all remaining variant information.

The decomposition is unique up to equivalence under measurable isomorphisms.

§6

Interpretation of the Invariant Core

The invariant core \(\phi_f\) is the coarsest representation that preserves all transformation-stable information encoded by \(f\). It induces an equivalence relation over inputs:

Two inputs are equivalent if they map to the same invariant representation under \(\phi_f\).

Thus, the invariant core defines a partition of the input space into invariance classes induced by the representation.

§7

Illustrative Examples

We ground the abstract decomposition in two failure modes that are well documented in medical image analysis: stain variation in digital pathology and acquisition-orientation variation in radiology. Both are nuisance transformations a clinically useful encoder is expected to absorb.

7.1 - Stain-Invariant Encoder (Histopathology)

The same tissue section under standard, pale, and deep H&E stain calibration - a known source of cross-scanner and cross-lab variation, collapses to a single invariant point. Inference: invariant to stain intensity and hue; preserves nuclear arrangement and tissue architecture.

7.2 - Orientation-Invariant Encoder (Radiology)

The same chest radiograph at upright, tilted, and rotated patient positioning - a routine acquisition artifact, collapses to a single invariant point. Inference: invariant to patient rotation and framing; preserves lung field anatomy and density.

7.3 - Benchmark Equivalence vs. Structural Difference

Consider two tumor-malignancy classifiers, A and B, trained on the same histopathology cohort and reporting comparable held-out AUC. Aggregate performance alone cannot tell them apart.

Although performance is similar, TRI distinguishes representations by their invariant content, and that content, not the benchmark score, determines which deployment conditions each model can be trusted under.

§8

Toward an Algebra of Representations

The invariant core is the first component of a broader theoretical framework. Subsequent releases of TRI develop:

canonical representations representation factorization invariant lattices kernel duality invariant comparison metrics universal invariance principles theoretical limits of representation learning

Together, these components aim to establish an algebraic theory of learned representations grounded in invariance structure.

§9

Conclusion

Closing Statement
A representation should be understood by the invariant information it preserves, not only by the tasks it solves.

Representation learning has largely been evaluated through downstream behavior. TRI proposes a complementary perspective, grounded in the claim that every representation admits a canonical invariant core capturing its transformation-stable information.

This release introduces the foundational decomposition and motivates a broader mathematical framework for studying learned representations through invariance structure.
